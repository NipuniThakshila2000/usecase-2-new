This Is Pre-Decision Moral Foresight

Multi-Stakeholder Scenario Sandbox

A government, NGO, company, ministry, or public institution can place a draft policy, speech, campaign, law, reform, or public message into the sandbox before launch. CIQ then tests how that proposal may be morally interpreted by different evidence-weighted ECC Profiles.

  • Will this build trust, or trigger mistrust?
  • Which communities may feel unheard, misrepresented, or violated?
  • Where are dignity, religious conscience, gender, shame, honour, safety, justice, or identity sensitivities?
  • How can the language be adjusted before harm is caused?
  • What kind of dialogue is needed before implementation?

The Sandbox is not generic roleplay. It is structured moral-risk modelling: agents respond through ECC profiles shaped by moral foundations, values hierarchy, justice orientation, institutional trust, sacred-value sensitivity, and identity-threat reactivity.

ECC Composite Engine

The ECC Composite Engine is a multi-instrument moral-psychology scoring layer for digital-twin simulations. It combines validated domains from moral, ethical, social, and political psychology instead of depending on one moral theory.

The output is an evidence-weighted ECC profile, not a clinical diagnosis or final judgment about a community. It gives a pre-dialogue signal so institutions can prepare to listen better.

A generic LLM can simulate a stakeholder. CIQ profiles the stakeholder through a structured moral-psychology framework before simulation begins.

CCC Orchestrator

The CCC Orchestrator compares multiple ECC profiles and identifies shared civic values, tensions, translation points, conflict risks, dialogue priorities, and policy redesign options.

Its central question is: where can communities remain distinct and still belong to one civic order?

The CCC Orchestrator helps identify where moral pluralism can be converted into common civic trust rather than fragmentation.

The Challenge

When Institutions Misread Community Conscience

A policy may be technically correct but fail because it ignores dignity, modesty, religious conscience, historical memory, gender sensitivity, institutional mistrust, or local concepts of fairness.

When institutions ignore how communities understand dignity, shame, honour, safety, fairness, justice, corruption, and trust, they create epistemic injustice.

Epistemic injustice occurs when a community's knowledge, testimony, experience, or moral language is dismissed, misunderstood, or excluded from decision-making.

The CIQ Civic Conscience Framework

The Four-Part CIQ Engine

1. ECC Profiler

The ECC Profiler creates stakeholder and digital-twin moral profiles using a psychometric ensemble across moral foundations, values hierarchy, justice orientation, authority orientation, institutional trust, pluralism tolerance, grievance sensitivity, and related civic-risk dimensions.

  • ECC Composite Score
  • Moral Coherence
  • Pluralism Capacity
  • Authority Sensitivity
  • Sacred-Value Sensitivity
  • Identity-Threat Reactivity

2. Red-Line Mapper

The Red-Line Mapper identifies what each actor experiences as violation, insult, threat, betrayal, injustice, corruption, exclusion, or sacred-value breach.

  • Public language versus private concern
  • Hidden fears and acceptable compromises
  • Likely alliance formation
  • Escalation paths and red-line breaches
  • Trust repair language
  • Dialogue conditions before implementation

3. Scenario Simulator

The Scenario Simulator runs multi-agent dialogue and policy simulations based on ECC profiles, then compares first reactions, moral objections, public narratives, private anxieties, and reconciliation pathways.

4. Outcome Calibrator

CIQ begins with synthetic modelling for early rehearsal, then improves through expert panels, stakeholder interviews, survey data, consultation transcripts, and observed outcomes.

Example

Policy Sensitivity Begins With Moral Context

An epistemically just system asks what a community understands as dignity, modesty, safety, trust, and appropriate care before labeling behavior as non-compliance.

01

Gender-Sensitive Care

In some contexts, women may avoid clinics where male doctors or male nurses are predominant. A purely administrative system may call this low compliance, poor awareness, or lack of access. CIQ asks which dignity, modesty, safety, and trust conditions must exist for care to be accepted.

02

Religious Conscience

During the COVID-19 pandemic, Sri Lanka's mandatory cremation policy created deep distress among Muslim communities whose religious conscience required burial practices. The issue became a question of dignity, trust, religious recognition, and whether affected communities felt heard by the state.
